Analysis of Refugee Migration Patterns

Insights for Aid Organizations and Policy Makers

Worldwide, more than 68 million people are forcibly displaced.
About 40 million of them are internally displaced, 25 million are refugees and 3 million are asylum-seekers.
Source: UNHCR

This project analyzes data on migration patterns of persons of concern. Our aim is to create new insights that are useful for aid organizations and policy makers alike.

The project is part of the "Network Analysis" course of the University of Helsinki.
The source code can be found on GitHub.

Summary

In our analysis of refugee migration patterns, we collected data and represented it as a network that you can see on the map. Countries are connected if a sufficiently high number of people flee from one country to another. You can change the threshold for "sufficiently high" with the threshold slider. Similarly, you can change the year you want to look at.

We found few correlations between our network measures and indices. Surprisingly, we did not see a correlation between the number of refugees leaving a country and the Human Development Index (HDI). A low HDI could be expected to lead to a higher number of refugees, but this does not appear to be the case. However, the network also does not fit a random graph model.

If you look at Syria, the country with the highest absolute number of refugees in these years, you can see that its HDI declined since the beginning of the civil war in 2011. There are, however, still a lot of countries with a lower HDI and lower numbers of refugees. This is because the HDI has trouble capturing short-term developments. The reason for the increased number of refugees are thus fairly short-term developments such as the Syrian civil war that has caused millions of people to flee their home.
